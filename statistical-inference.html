Statistical inference

Inference turns observations into statements about parameters, predictions, or decisions— while being explicit about assumptions, variability, and what could go wrong if the model is misspecified.

Estimation

Point estimators compress data into a best guess: method of moments, maximum likelihood, and Bayesian posteriors each encode different priorities (computation, optimality under a model, or prior knowledge).

  • Bias, variance, and the bias–variance tradeoff in prediction
  • Consistency: what happens as sample size grows
  • Robustness when likelihoods are imperfect

Uncertainty quantification

Confidence intervals and credible intervals answer different questions but both aim to communicate range—not certainty—around an estimate. Standard errors, bootstrap distributions, and posterior spreads are practical ways to obtain them.

Testing and decisions

Hypothesis tests formalize evidence against a null model; p-values and error rates should be read alongside effect sizes and study design. For decisions, prediction risk and utility matter as much as statistical significance.
