Statistical models & methods

A model is a deliberate simplification: it names variables, relationships, and error structure so inference and prediction have a target. Methods are the algorithms and diagnostics that fit the model to data responsibly.

Classical regression and extensions

Linear and generalized linear models connect predictors to responses through a systematic component plus a noise model. Diagnostics—residual plots, influence measures, and specification tests—keep the linear story honest.

  • ANOVA as structured linear models
  • Logistic and count models for non-Gaussian outcomes
  • Regularization (ridge, lasso) when dimensionality grows

Multilevel and hierarchical structure

When observations cluster (students in schools, repeated measures on people), partial pooling shares strength across groups and avoids overfitting idiosyncratic noise.

Modern statistical learning

Flexible methods—trees, ensembles, neural networks—reduce bias at the cost of variance and interpretability. Cross-validation, calibration, and external validation translate in-sample fit into claims that travel to new environments.
