Probability

Probability quantifies uncertainty. It gives rules for combining events, updating beliefs when information arrives, and describing how averages behave as evidence accumulates.

Probability

A probability space (Ω, ℱ, P) pairs a sample space of outcomes with a σ-algebra of measurable events and a measure P that assigns each event a number in [0, 1]. Kolmogorov’s axioms—non-negativity, normalization P(Ω) = 1, and countable additivity—keep the calculus coherent even when events become infinite unions.

Conditional probability rescales the measure to a known event: P(A | B) = P(A ∩ B) / P(B) when P(B) > 0. Bayes’ rule reverses the direction of conditioning, and the law of total probability decomposes an event across a partition.

  • Independence: P(A ∩ B) = P(A) P(B)
  • Bayes’ rule: P(A | B) = P(B | A) P(A) / P(B)
  • Inclusion–exclusion for unions of overlapping events

Random variables

A random variable X : Ω → ℝ is a measurable function that turns outcomes into numbers. Its distribution is summarized by the cumulative distribution function F(x) = P(X ≤ x), which is non-decreasing, right-continuous, and has limits 0 and 1.

Discrete variables carry a probability mass function p(x) = P(X = x); absolutely continuous variables carry a density f with P(X ∈ A) = ∫_A f(x) dx. Joint, marginal, and conditional distributions extend the same ideas to random vectors, with independence meaning the joint factors into marginals.

  • Bernoulli, Binomial, Poisson for counts and events
  • Normal, Exponential, Gamma, Beta for continuous magnitudes
  • Transformations via change of variables and the Jacobian

Expectation

Expectation is the integral of a random variable against its distribution: E[X] = ∫ x dF(x). It is linear— E[aX + bY] = a E[X] + b E[Y]—regardless of dependence, which makes it the workhorse for reasoning about averages.

Variance Var(X) = E[(X − E[X])²] measures spread; covariance and correlation measure co-movement. The law of the unconscious statistician gives E[g(X)] = ∫ g(x) dF(x), and the tower property E[X] = E[E[X | Y]] lets us compute expectations by conditioning on whatever structure is convenient.

  • Moments, moment generating functions, and characteristic functions
  • Conditional expectation as the best mean-square predictor
  • Jensen’s inequality: φ(E[X]) ≤ E[φ(X)] for convex φ

Inequalities

Inequalities bound probabilities and expectations when exact distributions are out of reach. They are the glue that turns moment information into tail control, and tail control into convergence.

  • Markov: P(X ≥ a) ≤ E[X] / a for X ≥ 0, a > 0.
  • Chebyshev: P(|X − μ| ≥ kσ) ≤ 1/k², a distribution-free spread bound.
  • Cauchy–Schwarz: E[XY]² ≤ E[X²] E[Y²], the probabilistic twin of the inner-product inequality.
  • Hoeffding: sub-Gaussian tail bound for bounded, independent sums— foundational for concentration.
  • Jensen: convex functions push expectation up, concave functions pull it down.

Convergence of random variables

Because random variables are functions, they can converge in several inequivalent senses, and picking the right mode matters for what a limit actually says.

  • Almost surely: P(X_n → X) = 1—convergence happens on the full probability set.
  • In probability: P(|X_n − X| > ε) → 0 for every ε > 0.
  • In Lp: E[|X_n − X|^p] → 0, strongest when p is large.
  • In distribution: F_n(x) → F(x) at continuity points of F—only the laws agree, not the variables.

Almost sure and Lp convergence imply convergence in probability, which implies convergence in distribution; the reverse directions fail in general. The two headline theorems sit on top of this ladder: the law of large numbers gives X̄_n → μ, and the central limit theorem gives √n (X̄_n − μ) / σ ⇒ N(0, 1)—the reason Gaussian approximations show up everywhere in inference.
