Probability

Probability quantifies uncertainty. It gives rules for combining events, updating beliefs when information arrives, and describing how averages behave as evidence accumulates.

Probability

A probability space $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)$ pairs a sample space of outcomes with a $\sigma$-algebra of measurable events and a measure $P$ that assigns each event a number in $[0, 1]$. Kolmogorov’s axioms—non-negativity, normalization $P(\Omega) = 1$, and countable additivity—keep the calculus coherent even when events become infinite unions.

Conditional probability rescales the measure to a known event: $$P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)}, \qquad P(B) > 0.$$ Bayes’ rule reverses the direction of conditioning, and the law of total probability decomposes an event across a partition.

  • Inclusion–exclusion: $$P\!\Bigl(\bigcup_{i=1}^{n} E_i\Bigr) = \sum_i P(E_i) - \sum_{i\lt j} P(E_i \cap E_j) + \sum_{i\lt j\lt k} P(E_i \cap E_j \cap E_k) - \cdots$$
  • Chain rule: $P(\bigcap_{i=1}^{n} E_i) = P(E_1)\, P(E_2 \mid E_1)\, P(E_3 \mid E_1 \cap E_2) \cdots P(E_n \mid E_1 \cap \cdots \cap E_{n-1})$
  • Law of total probability: $P(F) = \sum_i P(F \mid E_i)\, P(E_i)$; e.g. $P(A) = P(A \mid B)\,P(B) + P(A \mid B^c)\,P(B^c)$.
  • Independence: $E \perp F \iff P(E \cap F) = P(E)\, P(F)$.
  • Bayes: $$P(E_j \mid F) = \frac{P(F \mid E_j)\, P(E_j)}{\sum_i P(F \mid E_i)\, P(E_i)}.$$
  • Geometric series (useful for “first success” events): $\sum_{k=0}^{n} r^k = \dfrac{1 - r^{n+1}}{1 - r}$, and $\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} r^k = \dfrac{1}{1 - r}$ for $|r| \lt 1$.

Random variables

A random variable $X : \Omega \to \mathbb{R}$ is a measurable function that turns outcomes into numbers. Its distribution is summarized by the cumulative distribution function $F(x) = P(X \le x)$, which is non-decreasing, right-continuous, and has limits $0$ and $1$.

Discrete variables carry a probability mass function $p(x) = P(X = x)$; absolutely continuous variables carry a density $f$ with $$P(X \in A) = \int_A f(x)\, dx, \qquad f(x) = \frac{dF(x)}{dx}.$$ Joint, marginal, and conditional distributions extend the same ideas to random vectors, with independence meaning the joint factors into marginals.

Discrete families

  • Bernoulli $X \sim \operatorname{Bern}(p)$: $f(1) = p,\ f(0) = 1 - p$; $E[X] = p,\ \operatorname{Var}(X) = p(1 - p)$.
  • Binomial $X \sim \operatorname{Bin}(n, p)$: $f(x) = \binom{n}{x} p^x (1 - p)^{n-x}$; $E[X] = np,\ \operatorname{Var}(X) = np(1 - p)$. Sums of independent Binomials with the same $p$ add: $\operatorname{Bin}(n_1, p) + \operatorname{Bin}(n_2, p) = \operatorname{Bin}(n_1 + n_2, p)$.
  • Geometric $X \sim \operatorname{Geom}(p)$ (trial of first success): $f(x) = (1 - p)^{x-1} p$; $E[X] = 1/p,\ \operatorname{Var}(X) = (1 - p)/p^2$.
  • Negative Binomial $X \sim \operatorname{NB}(r, p)$ (trial of $r$th success): $f(x) = \binom{x-1}{r-1} (1 - p)^{x-r} p^r$; $E[X] = r/p,\ \operatorname{Var}(X) = r(1 - p)/p^2$.
  • Hypergeometric $X \sim \operatorname{HG}(N, K, n)$ (draw $n$ without replacement from $N$, of which $K$ are successes): $f(x) = \dfrac{\binom{K}{x}\binom{N - K}{n - x}}{\binom{N}{n}}$; $E[X] = n K / N$.
  • Poisson $X \sim \operatorname{Poisson}(\lambda)$: $f(x) = \dfrac{e^{-\lambda} \lambda^x}{x!}$; $E[X] = \operatorname{Var}(X) = \lambda$. Independent Poissons add: $\operatorname{Poisson}(\lambda_x) + \operatorname{Poisson}(\lambda_y) = \operatorname{Poisson}(\lambda_x + \lambda_y)$.

Continuous families

  • Uniform $X \sim \operatorname{Unif}(a, b)$: $f(x) = \tfrac{1}{b - a}$; $F(x) = \tfrac{x - a}{b - a}$; $E[X] = \tfrac{a + b}{2},\ \operatorname{Var}(X) = \tfrac{(b - a)^2}{12}$.
  • Exponential $X \sim \operatorname{Exp}(\lambda)$ (waiting time for one event): $f(x) = \lambda e^{-\lambda x}$, $F(x) = 1 - e^{-\lambda x}$ for $x \ge 0$; $E[X] = 1/\lambda,\ \operatorname{Var}(X) = 1/\lambda^2$.
  • Gamma $X \sim \operatorname{Gamma}(\alpha, \lambda)$ (waiting time for $\alpha$ events): $f(x) = \dfrac{\lambda^{\alpha}}{\Gamma(\alpha)}\, x^{\alpha - 1} e^{-\lambda x}$; $E[X] = \alpha/\lambda,\ \operatorname{Var}(X) = \alpha/\lambda^2$. For integer $\alpha$, $\Gamma(\alpha) = (\alpha - 1)!$.
  • Normal $X \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)$: $f(x) = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\,\sigma} \exp\!\left(-\dfrac{(x - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)$; $E[X] = \mu,\ \operatorname{Var}(X) = \sigma^2$. Affine closure: $Y = \alpha X + \beta \implies Y \sim N(\alpha \mu + \beta,\ \alpha^2 \sigma^2)$.

Jointly distributed variables

  • Joint CDF $F_{X,Y}(a, b) = P(X \le a, Y \le b)$, with marginals $F_X(a) = F_{X,Y}(a, \infty)$ and $F_Y(b) = F_{X,Y}(\infty, b)$.
  • Discrete marginal: $f_X(x) = \sum_y f_{X,Y}(x, y)$; continuous marginal: $f_X(x) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_{X,Y}(x, y)\, dy$.
  • Independence: $f_{X,Y}(x, y) = f_X(x)\, f_Y(y)$, equivalently the CDF factors. Under independence $E[g(X)\, h(Y)] = E[g(X)]\, E[h(Y)]$.
  • Extremes of i.i.d. samples with CDF $F$: $P(\max_i X_i \le m) = F(m)^n,\ P(\min_i X_i \le m) = 1 - (1 - F(m))^n.$
  • Change of variables: $f_{Y_1, Y_2}(y_1, y_2) = f_{X_1, X_2}(x_1, x_2)\,\left|\det \dfrac{\partial(x_1, x_2)}{\partial(y_1, y_2)}\right|.$

Expectation

Expectation is the integral of a random variable against its distribution: $$E[X] = \int x\, dF(x).$$ It is linear—$E[aX + bY] = a\, E[X] + b\, E[Y]$—regardless of dependence, which makes it the workhorse for reasoning about averages.

Variance $$\operatorname{Var}(X) = E\!\left[(X - E[X])^2\right] = E[X^2] - (E[X])^2$$ measures spread; if $\operatorname{Var}(X) = 0$ then $X = E[X]$ almost surely. Covariance $$\operatorname{Cov}(X, Y) = E[(X - E[X])(Y - E[Y])] = E[XY] - E[X]\, E[Y]$$ measures co-movement and vanishes when $X \perp Y$ (but not conversely). For sums, $$\operatorname{Var}\!\Bigl(\sum_{i=1}^{n} X_i\Bigr) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \operatorname{Var}(X_i) + \sum_{i \ne j} \operatorname{Cov}(X_i, X_j).$$

The law of the unconscious statistician gives $E[g(X)] = \int g(x)\, dF(x)$, and the tower property $E[X] = E\!\left[E[X \mid Y]\right]$ lets us compute expectations by conditioning. For an i.i.d. sample with mean $\mu$ and variance $\sigma^2$, $E[\bar{X}] = \mu$ and $\operatorname{Var}(\bar{X}) = \sigma^2/n$.

Moment generating functions

The MGF $\varphi(t) = E[e^{tX}]$ encodes all moments in one object: $$\varphi(t) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{E[X^n]}{n!}\, t^n, \qquad \left.\frac{\partial^n \varphi(t)}{\partial t^n}\right|_{t = 0} = E[X^n].$$ When $\varphi$ exists in a neighborhood of $0$ it determines the distribution uniquely.

  • Conditional expectation as the best mean-square predictor
  • Jensen’s inequality: $\varphi(E[X]) \le E[\varphi(X)]$ for convex $\varphi$

Inequalities

Inequalities bound probabilities and expectations when exact distributions are out of reach. They are the glue that turns moment information into tail control, and tail control into convergence.

  • Markov: for $X \ge 0$ and $a > 0$, $$P(X \ge a) \le \frac{E[X]}{a}.$$
  • Chebyshev: for any $k > 0$, $$P(|X - \mu| \ge k) \le \frac{\sigma^2}{k^2}.$$
  • One-sided Chebyshev (Cantelli): $$P(X - \mu \ge k) \le \frac{\sigma^2}{\sigma^2 + k^2}, \qquad k > 0.$$
  • Cauchy–Schwarz: $(E[YZ])^2 \le E[Y^2]\, E[Z^2]$.
  • Jensen: for convex $f$, $E[f(X)] \ge f(E[X])$; concave flips the direction. (Convex $\cup$, concave $\cap$.)
  • Hoeffding: sub-Gaussian tail bound for bounded, independent sums— foundational for concentration.

Convergence of random variables

Because random variables are functions, they can converge in several inequivalent senses, and picking the right mode matters for what a limit actually says.

  • Almost surely: $P(X_n \to X) = 1$—convergence happens on the full probability set.
  • In probability: $P(|X_n - X| > \varepsilon) \to 0$ for every $\varepsilon > 0$.
  • In $L^p$: $E[\,|X_n - X|^p\,] \to 0$, strongest when $p$ is large.
  • In distribution: $F_n(x) \to F(x)$ at continuity points of $F$—only the laws agree, not the variables.

Almost sure and $L^p$ convergence imply convergence in probability, which implies convergence in distribution; the reverse directions fail in general.

Law of large numbers (i.i.d. with $E[X_i] = \mu$): $$\text{WLLN:}\quad P(|\bar{X} - \mu| \ge \varepsilon) \to 0, \qquad \text{SLLN:}\quad P\!\left(\lim_{n \to \infty} \bar{X} = \mu\right) = 1.$$ SLLN equivalently says, for any $\varepsilon > 0$, $P(|\bar{X} - \mu| \ge \varepsilon \text{ i.o.}) = 0$: after some point the deviation stops happening.

Central limit theorem (finite variance): $$\frac{\bar{X} - \mu}{\sigma / \sqrt{n}} \;\Rightarrow\; N(0, 1) \quad \text{as } n \to \infty.$$ This is why Gaussian approximations show up everywhere in inference.
